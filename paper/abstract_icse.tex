\title{What Research Software do Computer Scientists build? A Typology
and Corpus Study of the Lecture Notes in Informatics}
\author{}
\date{}

\begin{document}
\maketitle
\begin{abstract}
RSE research has concentrated on the STEM and natural-science domains
that \emph{consume} research software and largely overlooked
\textbf{software engineering itself} as a discipline that
\emph{produces} it --- even though that is where software is most
tightly bound to engineering practice. We propose an empirical typology
of this field's software, grounded in the German-language \emph{Lecture
Notes in Informatics} (LNI) corpus of \textasciitilde14,000 papers. Each
paper is gated on whether it contains research software, then coded
along five dimensions: position in the research process,
software-lifecycle phase, type of software, techstack, and mode of
evaluation. The scheme is bootstrapped by an LLM, narrowed by human
review, and validated by two coders against a goldstandard (intercoder
reliability per dimension); the full corpus is then annotated by
multiple LLMs with majority voting. We find that computer-science
research software is reported mainly as a \emph{result} of the research
process rather than as data-analysis machinery, and that
project-definition and deployment phases are underrepresented in the
reported lifecycle. We release the typology, prompts, and pipeline for
replication.
\end{abstract}
